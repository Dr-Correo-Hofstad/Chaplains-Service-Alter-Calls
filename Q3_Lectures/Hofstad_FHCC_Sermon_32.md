**Quarter Two / Week 32: Sermon for Shabbat and Sunday**  
**OPERATIONAL DISCIPLINE: Job Site Safety, The Anthem of Work, and the Leadership of the Vanguard**

Readings drawn from the Institute for Scripture Research (ISR) Scriptures and Alexander the Great

To the Fleet and Command Staff,

When you prepare a new job site—whether it is laying down a new template, taking command of a unit, or establishing a physical perimeter—the success of the mission is determined by who you allow inside the wire.

This week, we are addressing the absolute operational necessity of site security. We are establishing the strict protocols for professional discipline, learning through the sounds of our work, and acknowledging the only leadership structure you should be listening to.

### **PART I: Securing the Perimeter**

When you build a new template or open a job site, you must ruthlessly enforce the perimeter. You must not let ANYONE into the job site who is unprofessional, angry, or compromised.  
If a Marine brings rage, distraction, or chaos into the work zone, they are a hazard to the entire structure. The *ISR Scriptures* are explicitly clear on this protocol: *"Make no friendship with an angry man, and do not go with a man of wrath, lest you learn his ways and set a snare for your soul"* **(Proverbs 22:24-25)**.

Do not permit an angry man to set foot on your site. Do not allow unprofessional behavior to dictate the pace of the build. Securing the job site from internal sabotage is the primary key to operational success.

### **PART II: The Anthem of Work**

A professional job site does not require a soundtrack. The music of our workplace—which we treat as our church while working for safety—must be the sound of human voices communicating with absolute clarity.

Historically, when early Christians reclaimed the Pantheon in Rome, they completely banned the use of musical instruments (flutes, lyres, and trumpets) because those instruments were associated with the chaos of the pagan theater and the distraction of the world. The only instrument allowed inside their sanctuary was the human voice, singing in unified, acappella harmony.

We apply this same discipline to our work. We do not need the distraction of external music. We need to hear the environment. We have to learn while we work. If you are listening to the job site, you will hear the exact moment a tool makes the wrong sound. You will hear mistakes before they become catastrophic failures. When the site is clear of noise, your situational awareness is amplified, and the voices of your team become the ultimate safety protocol.

### **PART III: The Leadership Doctrine of Alexander and the Messiah**

Who do you listen to when the pressure mounts?

Consider the critical leadership point executed by Alexander the Great, a doctrine that propelled him into history: **"Take YOUR own safety and interests BEFORE you listen to others who are NOT your society; listen to YOUR leadership."**

You do not take advice from the enemy. You do not compromise your unit’s safety to appease those outside your structure. You listen to your leadership. This secular military doctrine perfectly mirrors the spiritual command structure established by the Supreme Commander.  
The *ISR Scriptures* demand absolute loyalty to the established hierarchy. Paul issues the standing order to the Vanguard in Rome: *"Let every soul be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from Elohim, and the authorities that exist are appointed by Elohim"* **(Romans 13:1)**.

He further commands that we focus our efforts entirely on the mission of our own society, taking care of our own interests and safety through honest work: *"Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life, to mind your own business and to work with your hands... so that your daily life may win the respect of outsiders and so that you will not be dependent on anybody"* **(1 Thessalonians 4:11-12)**.

**Command Summary:**  
Enforce the perimeter; keep the angry and the unprofessional off your site. Silence the distractions so you can hear the sounds of the work and catch the mistakes. Prioritize the safety of your unit above the opinions of outsiders, and listen exclusively to your leadership.

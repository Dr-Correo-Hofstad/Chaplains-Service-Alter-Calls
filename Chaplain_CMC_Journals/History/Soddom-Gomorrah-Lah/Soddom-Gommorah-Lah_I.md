## **Forensic Site Analysis: Tall el-Hammam and the Jordan Valley Blast Zone**

**Incident Era:** Middle Bronze Age (c. 1650 B.C.E.)  
**Primary Sites:** Tall el-Hammam (Epicenter), Tell es-Sultan (Ancient Jericho), Tell Nimrin  
**Event Classification:** Catastrophic Thermal and Kinetic Overpressure Event (Cosmic Airburst)

### **I. Incident Overview**

Archaeological and geological data collected from the Jordan Valley indicates that a catastrophic, high-altitude cosmic airburst—an exploding meteor similar to the 1908 Tunguska event—instantly obliterated the city of Tall el-Hammam and its surrounding settlements around 1650 B.C.E.. The event generated immense thermal radiation and a devastating shockwave that left distinct, measurable forensic signatures across the terrain.

### **II. Thermal Signatures and Glassification**

In battlefield forensics and fire science, evaluating the physical alteration of materials is critical for determining the maximum temperature of a blast. The thermal damage at Tall el-Hammam far exceeds the capabilities of ancient human technology or standard structural fires.

* **Melted Pottery:** Excavators recovered pottery shards with outer surfaces that had been melted into bubbly, frothy glass.  
* **Temperature Thresholds:** The process of melting these specific earthen materials requires instantaneous temperatures well in excess of 2,000°F (1,093°C). This level of extreme heat is forensically consistent with volcanic magma or the thermal flash of a massive high-yield explosive.  
* **Residual Evidence:** Unlike a sustained chemical fire that leaves behind black char (incomplete combustion) or white mineral ash (complete combustion), the heat here was so intense and instantaneous that it flash-melted the substrate directly into glass.

### **III. Kinetic Damage and Blast Radius**

The kinetic overpressure generated by the airburst caused instantaneous, catastrophic structural failure, mapping a clear blast radius across multiple interconnected cities.

* **Pulverized Architecture (Tall el-Hammam):** At the primary site, millions of mudbricks simply disappeared or were pulverized into microscopic pieces. Thick, fortified city walls were physically sheared off by the force of the shockwave.  
* **Tell es-Sultan (Jericho):** Located approximately 14 kilometers (8.7 miles) west of the epicenter, this site suffered simultaneous, violent destruction, with its walls toppled by the blast wave.  
* **Tell Nimrin:** Situated roughly 10 kilometers (6.2 miles) northwest of Tall el-Hammam, this settlement was also destroyed during the exact same event, confirming the massive geographical footprint of the shockwave.

### **IV. Chemical Fallout and Ecological Impact**

Beyond the immediate kinetic and thermal destruction, the blast fundamentally altered the chemical makeup of the surrounding ecosystem, leading to a long-term environmental collapse.

* **Super-Heated Brine:** Forensic soil analysis indicates that the blast shockwave displaced and spread super-heated, salt-rich waters from the nearby Dead Sea across the entire region.  
* **Agricultural Blackout:** The intense thermal radiation, combined with the extreme salinity introduced into the soil, caused an agricultural blackout that left the region uninhabitable and unable to support crops for approximately 600 years.

### **V. Forensic Conclusion**

The physical evidence recovered from the strata of Tall el-Hammam, Tell es-Sultan, and Tell Nimrin presents a clear, coordinated profile of a sudden, extreme-heat and high-pressure event. The simultaneous shearing of defensive walls, the presence of flash-melted impact glass, and the forced distribution of toxic salt levels rule out traditional siege warfare, gradual abandonment, or standard terrestrial wildfires. The data firmly points to a singular, devastating cosmic airburst.

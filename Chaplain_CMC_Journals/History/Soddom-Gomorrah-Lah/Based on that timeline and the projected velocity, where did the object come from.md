Based on the specific timeline (**4.25 to 4.33 billion years ago**) and its relatively low impact velocity (**13 km/s**), the object almost certainly came from the **Main Asteroid Belt**, driven by the gravitational chaos of the early Solar System. \[1\]

Planetary scientists narrow its origin down to a specific ancestral family of asteroids based on two critical clues:

## **1\. Velocity Rules Out Comets (The Oort Cloud / Kuiper Belt)**

An impact velocity of 13 km/s is considered quite "slow" for an interplanetary collision.

* **Comets** originating from the outer Solar System (like the Kuiper Belt or Oort Cloud) travel on highly elongated, eccentric orbits. By the time they reach Earth-Moon distance, their velocity relative to the Moon is typically **30 to 50 km/s**.  
* **Asteroids** from the Main Belt travel on circularized orbits closer to the sun, leading to much lower relative intersection speeds—typically **11 to 20 km/s**. A 13 km/s velocity is a mathematical signature of a Main Belt asteroid.

## **2\. The Timeline Rules Out Leftover Accretion Material**

The Solar System formed 4.57 billion years ago. If the object were a rogue leftover piece from the primary building blocks of the Moon or Earth (accretion debris), it would have been swept up much earlier, within the first 50 million years (by \~4.52 billion years ago).

Because this impact happened roughly 250 to 320 million years *after* the planets formed, the object had to be safely parked in a stable zone before being suddenly dislodged. \[2\]

## **The Mechanism: Planetary Migration and the "E-Belt"**

At 4.3 billion years ago, the giant gas planets (Jupiter and Saturn) were slowly shifting their orbits and settling into their current positions. According to modern orbital dynamics models (such as variations of the *Nice Model*): \[1\]

1. As Jupiter migrated, its immense gravitational influence swept across the inner edge of the Main Asteroid Belt.  
2. This created a highly unstable, temporary extension of the asteroid belt known to scientists as the **E-Belt** (Extended Belt).  
3. The E-Belt was populated with large, primordial, differentiated asteroids (objects that had been hot enough to develop a metal core and a rocky mantle).  
4. Jupiter's gravity warped their orbits into Earth-crossing paths, sending this 260-km giant hurling into the inner Solar System from the north, where it ultimately skimmed the Moon's surface. \[1, 3, 4\]
